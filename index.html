Fonofone

Transmission & Innovation

Committed to artistic transmission, Daoust designed Fonofone (also known as Musicolateur)—a sound-creation instrument intended to awaken musical creativity in young people. Since 2004, this project has remained a vital part of his artistic and pedagogical engagement, echoing his belief that listening is a learned, creative act.

Biography

Internationally Renowned Composer and Pioneer of Electroacoustic Music

Born in Longueuil, Québec, Yves Daoust is one of the most distinctive and influential voices in contemporary electroacoustic music. His work stands at the crossroads of concert music, cinema, pedagogy, and sound research. Few composers have so consistently transformed listening itself into an artistic experience, positioning Daoust as a central figure in electroacoustic and mixed music on the world stage.

A decisive encounter with Maurice Blackburn led Daoust to the National Film Board of Canada (NFB), where he undertook specialized training in film music techniques. Immersed in the legendary Sound Workshop, he developed a refined sensitivity to sound-image relationships, narrative listening, and spatial perception. During this period, he notably collaborated with media theorist Marshall McLuhan, an encounter that profoundly shaped his thinking on media, perception, and the cognitive dimensions of sound.

Daoust pursued advanced training at the Institut international de musique électroacoustique de Bourges (IMEB), one of the world's most important centers for electroacoustic creation. At Bourges, he was deeply influenced by the artistic processes of Alain Savouret, particularly the concept of "virtual cinema," which treats sound as a dramaturgical space in itself. This approach became foundational to Daoust's aesthetic: music not merely as structure, but as a lived, cinematic experience for the ear.

In 1978, Daoust co-founded ACREQ (Association pour la création et la recherche électroacoustiques du Québec)—an organization that would later evolve into Elektra. Through concerts, site-specific events, and international forums, ACREQ played a decisive role in the global diffusion of electroacoustic music. Daoust's leadership helped establish Québec as a major hub of electroacoustic innovation.

In 1981, he was appointed professor at the Conservatoire de musique et d'art dramatique du Québec, mandated to design and implement comprehensive electroacoustic programs. His pedagogical vision shaped generations of composers and institutionalized electroacoustic composition as a rigorous artistic discipline.

Key Works and Artistic Trajectory

Since his professional debut in 1978, Daoust has composed at a remarkably steady pace—nearly one major work per year—across electroacoustic, mixed, instrumental, and interdisciplinary genres. Notable compositions include:

Paris, les Grands-Magasins (1976)

A landmark work using phonography and urban sounds to capture lived experience with cinematic intensity.

Quatuor (1979)

A foundational musique concrète piece grounded in culture, revisiting musical heritage through radical sound transformation.

Valse (1981)

A seminal mixed work for ensemble and tape, where instrumental and electroacoustic layers coexist in parallel discourses.

Chorals ornés (2007–2008)

A mature synthesis of his aesthetic, where nature and culture unfold simultaneously without fusion, affirming his unique compositional voice.

Recognition

In recognition of his lifelong contribution to musical creation, innovation, and transmission, Yves Daoust received the Prix Serge-Garant from the Fondation Émile-Nelligan in 2009.
